Introduction

The dataset I am working with is Spotify metadata for songs hosted on its website for streaming, ranging in values from key to “energy” for tracks with release dates from 1927 to 2023. I am studying what makes a song popular, particularly techno tracks. This particular idea stuck out to me, as I am a techno DJ outside of data science. As a DJ, it is always best to keep in tune with what is most popular with crowds at all times before performing, as it is your duty to keep your audience happy.

The dataset contains 114,000 rows and 21 columns of Spotify metadata. Spotify measures popularity, danceability, and energy for each track. Many features are algorithmically derived, including valence.

Bivariate Analysis

There is a weak negative relationship between valence and popularity in techno tracks: as valence increases, popularity tends to decrease slightly.

Interesting Aggregates

The 2010s appear to be the most popular decade for techno music, likely due to the global rise of EDM. This decade also shows lower valence and energy compared to others.

Assessment of Missingness

Tempo is the only column with missing values. Missingness may be NMAR because Spotify’s algorithm may struggle with complex or irregular rhythm structures.

However, missing tempo values also appear correlated with shorter track duration, suggesting MAR may be more likely.

Framing a Prediction

I use linear regression to predict song popularity using tempo, danceability, and energy. RMSE is used to evaluate performance because it penalizes large errors more heavily.

Baseline Model

The baseline linear regression model uses 11 quantitative features. It achieved: RMSE = 19.10 and R² = 0.0048.

The low R² suggests weak predictive power, likely due to noisy genre labeling and feature scale variation.

Final Model

The final model uses Ridge Regression with engineered features: energy × loudness and danceability ÷ tempo. Features were standardized and tuned using GridSearchCV.

The model improves slightly over baseline, achieving RMSE = 19.036.

Fairness Analysis

I evaluated fairness by comparing RMSE across low- and high-danceability songs using a permutation test. The observed statistic was -0.01 with a p-value of 0.233.

Since p > 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis, indicating no significant performance difference.
