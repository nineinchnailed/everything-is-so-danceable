ASSESSMENT OF MISSINGNESS

Tempo is the only column in my dataset that contains missing values. I believe tempo may be NMAR (Not Missing At Random) because the missingness could depend on the tempo value itself. For example, Spotify's tempo-detection algorithm may have difficulty estimating tempo for tracks with unusual rhythmic structures, highly variable tempos, ambient sections, or other characteristics directly related to the true tempo. If this is the case, the probability that a tempo value is missing would depend on the unobserved tempo itself, which is the definition of NMAR.

However, I also found that tracks with missing tempo values tend to be shorter than tracks with recorded tempo values. This suggests that missingness may be related to track duration. If Spotify requires a minimum song length to reliably estimate tempo, then the missingness could instead be explained by duration. In that case, the missingness would be MAR (Missing At Random) rather than NMAR, because it would depend on an observed characteristic of the track.

To better determine whether the tempo column is truly NMAR, I would want additional information about Spotify's audio-feature extraction process, such as confidence scores for tempo estimates, whether tempo detection failed during processing, or metadata describing why a tempo value is missing. Listening to some of the songs with missing tempos myself, I find that the driving drums of each of the tracks tend to be softer and lack heavy bass, which may hinder tempo recognition.

To investigate whether popular modern songs differ from popular older songs, I divided songs into two groups:

Group A: Songs released in 2016 or later with popularity ≥ 70.
Group B: Songs released before 2016 with popularity ≥ 70.

For each audio feature (energy, danceability, instrumentalness, and acousticness), I conducted a two-sample t-test.

Null Hypothesis (H0): The mean value of the feature is the same for modern and older popular songs.
Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The mean value of the feature differs between modern and older popular songs.

I used a significance level of α=0.01. Because four hypothesis tests were conducted simultaneously, I applied a Bonferroni correction to control the family-wise error rate. The corrected p-values are shown below.

Model Results

A two-sample t-test is appropriate because the goal is to compare the average value of a continuous audio feature between two independent groups of songs. The t-statistic measures the standardized difference in group means, making it suitable for detecting differences between modern and older popular songs. I chose 2016 as the cutoff year because music released within the last seven years is often considered modern, and 2016 represented the earliest year that fit this definition within the dataset.

After applying the Bonferroni correction, the tests for energy, danceability, and acousticness remained statistically significant because their adjusted p-values were below 0.01. The test for instrumentalness was not significant, so there is insufficient evidence to conclude that modern and older popular songs differ in instrumentalness.

These results suggest that modern popular songs tend to have lower energy, higher danceability, and higher acousticness than older popular songs. While the results provide evidence of differences between the groups, they do not prove that release era directly causes these differences, as other factors may influence a song's audio characteristics, considering that music trends feature numerous feedback loops.

FINAL MODEL

The final model builds on a baseline linear regression by adding two engineered features designed to better reflect how audio characteristics relate to song popularity. I added energy × loudness, which captures overall track intensity, since louder and more energetic tracks determine higher sonic impact in modern music production, working in synergy to amplify its effects. I added danceability/tempo, to capture how dance-friendly a song is relative to its speed, since songs with similar danceability can be much more tiring at higher BPMs than lower ones.

The model used Ridge Regression to handle multicollinearity among correlated audio features (e.g., energy, loudness, and danceability). All numeric features were standardized, and feature transformations were included within a single sklearn Pipeline to funnel smooth transitions.

Hyperparameters were tuned using 5-fold cross-validation with GridSearchCV, selecting the optimal alpha (regularization strength) from a set of values. The best alpha was determined by the lowest cross-validated RMSE.

Compared to the baseline linear regression model, the final model improves performance by using meaningful feature interactions, stabilizing coefficients with regularization, and improving generalization via cross-validation, resulting in a slightly lower test RMSE of 19.036.

FAIRNESS ANALYSIS

I evaluated the fairness of the final Ridge regression model by comparing its performance across two groups defined by danceability: Group X consists of songs below the median danceability (lower), and Group Y consists of songs at or above the median (upper). Danceability is meaningful as it reflects how suitable a song is for, believe it or not, dance, crucial to EDM and controlling a dancefloor’s energy.

As this is a regression task, I used RMSE as the evaluation metric.

Null hypothesis: The model is fair, and RMSE is the same for both groups; any observed difference is due to pure chance.
Alternative hypothesis: The model performs worse (higher RMSE) for low-danceability songs, and the difference in RMSE (low − high) shows a statistical significance level >= 0.05.

The test statistic will be RMSE (low − high), performing a permutation test of 1,000 simulations at a significance level of 0.05.

The observed statistic was -0.01 with a p-value of 0.233. Since the p-value is greater than 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. This suggests there is no statistically significant evidence that the model performs differently across low- and high-danceability songs, and any observed difference in RMSE is likely due to random chance.
